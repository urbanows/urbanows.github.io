Another non-native species is coming

The cactus family, Cactaceae, originated in the New World, remaining there until Europeans arrived and took specimens home. I’ve seen them potted on windowsills from Slovenia to Jerusalem. One foolish foreign introduction was to use pricklypear (Opuntia) for hedgerows in Australia. Within 40 years they covered 100 million acres. There were no natural enemies. The Australian government then introduced the cactus moth, Cactoblastis cactorum, from Argentina, which brought the prickly pears back to 10 million acres. This moth, which lays its eggs in “egg sticks” on the flat pads, was introduced other places for a similar purpose, including the Caribbean Island Nevis in 1956. From there, it arrived in Florida by natural dispersal or in horticultural imports in the 1990s. It will now eat its way across the southern states, spreading at an estimated 100 miles a year. It is projected to reach Texas in 2007, and the USDA has no funds to track it. The Lepidopterists’ Society News for Spring 2004 solicits volunteers to monitor the progress of this small, devastating beast.

There is little chance of any control, though The Nature Conservancy is attempting to protect some rare, Florida Opuntia by hand removing the eggs.. The cactus moth will change the Chihuahuan Desert. Now, we do have some native moths that consume prickly pear. Long ago I split the pad of a White Sands one for a herbarium collection and found a dozen white larvae alarmed at my disturbance. These were almost certainly different from Cactoblastis.. While their early instars are white, the large, late ones are bright orange with black spots.

In the Chihuahuan Desert we have unleashed changes for which we have no idea of the outcome. Early, heavy grazing removed grasses and disturbed the soil, allowing erosion and the spread of unpalatable species such as creosotebush, cholla, and pricklypear. This almost certainly made a state change, and we will never again see the primordial landscape. I am reminded of a scene in the early Mel Gibson movie Thunderdome. The hero has broken out of a post-nuclear settlement in a railway locomotive. Thundering across the Outback, the hero asks the locomotive driver, “What’s the plan?” The driver looks astonished, replying, “Plan? There ain’t no plan!” Our desert’s future is equally obscure.

Life Finds a Way

The sun had just risen over the Sacramento Mountains of New Mexicowhen my cinnamon roll made its escape. At least that what it looked like. Several hundred Harvester Ants were under it moving slowly toward their colony, their home. I had spent the night at White Sands National Monument, camped near the Alkali Flats, to monitor their activity. While some scouts are out in the heat of the day, they swarm out at dawn.

Species of Harvester Ants are widespread in the dry Southwest and other countries. They are famous for the severity of their bite, and are, at this moment, in a life and death battle with Fire Ants, as that alien species moves up the valley of the Rio Grande.

At White Sands in the active dunes you can see their scattered colonies, which are covered with small dark rocks. These rocks serve to stabilize the sand of the mound, and help keep the colony warm. The average deep soil temperature at White Sands is 60 degrees F, but a thermometer thrust in the single entrance hole reads about 98 F.

These ants make their living in an unusual way. Usually, harvester ants gather seeds, but you may have noticed there are few plants and seeds in the most active dunes at White Sands. There are even fewer at the edge of the Alkali Flats west of these dunes.

During the night the entrance to a colony is guarded by a few individuals with their pincers held upward. At dawn, the ants swarm out. They forage for insects that settle on the sands during the cold night. Sure, they will take the odd seed or cinnamon roll, but most of their diet is insects.

The insects are stored in chambers in the sand around the colony mound. In the one colony I checked, one chamber contained only Ladybug Beetles and one small moths called Millers.

A biologist often asks, “What’s the food chain, who eats who?” In that barren dune field, there are many Bleached Earless Lizards. I’ve often watched these small creatures lifting one foot then another off the warm sand between eating Harvester Ants, their main diet. What eats the lizards? I’m not sure. I do know that Kit Fox get out there. One summer I saw the tracks of a Kit Fox with a damaged right hind foot over an area of 20,000 acres of dunes.

Them darn ticks

Evolution and nature care not one whit about our comfort, so not everything in the rich tapestry of ecosystems is pleasant for us. That includes ticks. My first encounter with ticks was with friends while searching for a cave near the Current River in Missouri. As I pushed through heavy brush I glanced down to see hundreds of them crawling upwards on my Levi jacket and jeans. We spent that evening in camp picking them off and popping them in a carbide lamp’s hot flame.

Where are ticks? Almost everywhere, but especially (1) at slightly damp places with natural vegetation and (2) near the habitat of favorite hosts. There are about 850 tick species, and the US has 82 of them. They all live on blood. Mammals are the favorite of most adults, but larval and nymph stage, especially, may be found on birds and reptiles as well. Once, I was engaged in the grim task of shooting jackrabbits near Aden Crater for a student’s carrion arthropod study. As I dropped those poor critters into plastic bags I saw that every big ear carried 30 or more ticks.

Ticks have four life stages: egg, larvae, nymph and adult. The last three feed on blood. There are two main groups, hard ticks and soft ticks. (What are called seed ticks are larvae.) Mating is accomplished while adults are on the body of the host. Female hard ticks mate once, lay a huge batch of eggs, then die. The males die after mating. Soft ticks mate and lay smaller batches of eggs several times. Of course, ticks spend most of their life waiting for a host animal.

Ticks transmit 30 major diseases, 11 of them found in the US. These include: Lyme Disease, Ehrlichiosis (with two forms caused by different bacteria species), Babesiosis, Tick Paralysis, Tick-borne Relapsing Fever, Tularemia, Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever, Colorado Tick Fever, Masters Disease, and Southern Tick Associated Rash Illness. Fortunately, several of these are most common in other regions and most can be treated with antibiotics. However, tick paralysis is from a neurotoxin in the tick’s saliva. It is considered to be a threat only to children among humans.

The good news from that nasty list is that the tick usually transmits the disease only after being attached for a day or more. So, full body checks at the end of the day can really pay off. Also, of course, finding an engorged female tick—the size of an oblong, purple cherry with little, waving legs—in your hair is not a happy thing. I’ve found them like that on my dog even in winter.

The usual advice includes:
Wear light-colored clothing to spot ticks (good around Africanized bees, also),
Scan clothes and any exposed skin for ticks,
Stay on cleared trails,
Use insect repellant containing DEET,
Avoid sitting on the ground or on rock piles.

I’ve been lucky in the desert and have encountered few ticks, despite all those on the jackrabbits. But be aware. We are part of the ecosystem and an acceptable host to these wee creatures.

Their mother loves them

I stared in horror as an 8 inch Giant Desert Centipede (Scolopendra heros) rushed out of a crack near my fireplace. Moving like an implacable monster from one of the Aliens movies, it rushed toward my bare feet. (I have since read that it was running for cover, not attacking me.) I danced back and ran for the insect spray. A good washing in that made little change except for some writhing. I was not rid of it until I picked it up with bottle tongs and threw it out the front door of my desert house.

Centipedes have a world wide distribution. We have two centipede species here, the giant one--the largest centipede species--and the smaller Common Desert Centipede (Scolopendra polymorpha). They have similar habits, but, the Giant, with dark head and tail and straw-colored segments, is more distinctively colored.

These incredible creatures are said to be entirely nocturnal, but I have seen one in the wild in daytime at Franklin Mountains State Park, Texas. It was a cool, misty day, and their aversion to sunlight is supposed to be due to a lack of a waxy coat to impede water loss. Whatever its intentions, it moved quickly over the gravel surface, probing into every hole it found. They eat nearly any insect they find, sometimes even small lizards and mice.

If you see a Giant Desert Centipede, notice that the head and tail ends look similar. This is purported to be an adaptation to protect them from predators. If some hungry creature grabs the “head”, there’s a 50-50 chance the real head will swing back to inject venom from a pair of pincer-like appendages in front of the legs. That venom is reported to be very painful but not dangerous to humans. On the tail end, also, are two elongated, prehensile legs apparently used to grasp prey. Despite anecdotal stories the other legs, one pair per segment, have sharp claws but no venom.

Mother centipedes take good care of their offspring. They coil around the eggs and grooms them carefully. She does not feed during this time. The eggs hatch in three weeks, and the young are only three millimeters long. She tends them until their first molt about three weeks (some say a few days) later, then leaves. When I see these native predators—so important in our ecosystem, I am very glad they are only a few millimeters high at the shoulder.

The Good People of Istanbul

I went to Istanbul (and Beirut) in 1958 while on leave from the army in Germany

I explored the great city on foot. It's cheap and the only way to get the real feel of any city. I walked the Galata Bridge and saw Topkapi Palace, Sancta Sophia and the Blue Mosque.

Through all this I wore a shapeless, black flannel suit. I carried no camera, since it was my theory that you can see a place or take pictures, not both. I had a crew cut getting shaggy. My passport was at the dollar a night hotel.

But I was clearly not a Turk, and the mob I blundered into near a mosque knew it. It was a couple hundred men, many middle-aged. and they were shouting something angry about America.

They crowded in close, and one put his chest against mine. "American!" he shouted.

My wit, a few history classes, and a distant reading of T. E. Lawrence saved me. "Deutsch! Sprecken ze Deutsch?" I shouted back.

"German?" the man exalted in English.

I smiled broadly. "Jawohl, Ich bin ein Deutschlander." My accent would have gotten me jailed in Berlin.

The crowd cheered and patted me on the back and shoulders. There's nothing like sharing a defeat to build bonds of friendship.

I nodded, shook hands all around and walked off in the clearest direction I could see. I was soon completely lost--though still immensely relieved--in narrow, cart-wide streets. Worse, the sun went down and I could no longer tell directions. I wandered and wandered as the streets emptied of people and it grew dark. The only light was from dim, second floor windows. Either I was walking in circles, or there was no main street in the miles of this ancient ghetto. And I hadn’t eaten since breakfast.

After what seemed like hours of darkness I saw a lighted shop window ahead. I rushed to it and looked in through the dirty window, seeing bread and sweet rolls.

I pushed the door. It opened slightly, releasing a puff of yeasty, hot air. I was starving, and I was in a bakery! I went in to the clanging of a large bell over the door. A small, muscular man in slacks and undershirt came out of the back. He wiped flour-dusted hands on an apron as he came with an expectant look on his face.

I smiled and pointed to the bread in the window.

A look of disgust came over his face, and he shook his head emphatically, no!

I pointed again more forcefully and reached into my pocket for coins.

He shook his head again. No!

I looked pleadingly and saw comprehension come over his face. He grabbed my upper, right arm with a muscular, left hand that felt like steel. Without a word he marched me to the back of the shop and down a narrow corridor. We came into a stone-walled room with huge, brick ovens that may have been built in the days of the Romans. Yellow light flickered from the fire pits beneath them, and two, bare light bulbs only added to a feeling that we were somewhere deep in the earth. A dozen men silently kneaded dough and formed loaves.

The baker took a rag and threw open the iron door of one of the ovens. With a long, wooden paddle, he reached in and brought out a flattish, braided loaf. Smiling now, he held it out to me.

I juggled the hot bread for a minute, then twisted off a piece and tasted. "Fantastic!" I said with a huge smile.

He nodded seriously and handed me the loaf. I held out coins, and he took what may have amounted to two cents American.

Smiling now, he took me outside and pointed vigorously up the street the pointed right. I followed the directions and found the main street I'd lost. I was in bed in my hotel room in another hour.

The Rattler


Here's a Mojve rattler, a little bigger than the one that got me. Rattlesnakes are attacked and eaten by dozens of other creatures, and they nearly always match the soil around them in color.

Losing My Fear of Rattlesnakes.

Bill Reid

In the milliseconds before pain I felt a cool, wet mouth slap twice against my foot. But I'm ahead of myself. In meeting wildlife there is a difference between respect and fear. Respect is what you feel when near power lines. Fear comes when you hear a rattler very close. Long ago in Colorado I heard that buzzing, looked down, and saw coils writhing under my running shoe. That was horror. Later, I mentally made a deal: I wouldn't bite them, and they wouldn't bite me. It worked for a while

Living in New Mexico I found wildlife from centipedes to bobcats. And I saw rattlesnakes from six inches to six feet long. I've never killed one because of my deal. Also, they're dinner for a dozen other creatures and may be central to our desert ecosystems.

On an August evening, wearing cut-offs and flip flops, I lolled at the TV until I recalled I hadn't checked the horse's water. His two acres were in back, and I reached it by crossing rough grass and through a gate. I grabbed a flashlight.

The flashlight was weak, and the grass heads cast confusing shadows. As I reached the gate, I felt two wet slaps on my left foot, followed by a burning pain. I jumped back and swung the light around. I saw nothing, so I pointed the light down and saw four crimson droplets an inch back of the toes on my left foot. I scanned the light around and again saw nothing.

A rattlesnake, I asked myself? Maybe it was a scorpion, a velvet ant, or one of the huge tarantulas? (I'm good at denial.) Inside, it didn't look like much, but the burning intensified. I washed it, watched more blood ooze, and noticed a silver dollar-sized swelling. An allergic reaction? In ten more minutes my foot was rigid and numb except for the burning. It was time to get help.

I found my keys and clumped outside, one foot bare and one with a flip flop, and struggled into the truck. It took a moment to figure how to use the clutch, but I got the thing started and moved slowly to the gate. There, I got out and opened the gate. On the other side I went to close the gate and saw the house lights on and the wide open front door. It had to be shut, and the 300 foot walk in darkness was a long one.

I locked up and started for Las Cruces. I've got it made, I thought! Then, a hot flush swept over me. Instantly, sweat poured from my head and ran down my back. It dripped from my arms onto my legs. It slimed my hands as I steered the sharp turns to the highway. Shaking began, too, and I tried to focus on driving. I noticed each yard of travel over the 15 miles to Las Cruces. With just a little excitement, I got into the hospital lot and clumped my way to the E.R.

The receptionist looked up. "Can I help you?"

I looked down. My flaming-red foot was twice its usual size. "I think a rattlesnake bit me."

All the usual needles went in. Every fifteen minutes they'd measure my foot and write notes right on me in ball point pen. Short of amputation there was no chance of losing the notes! Two hours later, three small bottles added to the flow of saline from my second IV. I knew I'd received tetanus and antibiotic shots, and later learned I'd gotten coagulants and near the max for antivenin. My huge left foot was dramatically elevated on a padded rack. The swelling reached half way to my knee, and it's crimson deepened to a royal purple.

By then the nurse knew I was a biologist, and told snakebite stories each time she came by. The most fun was her explanation of how surgeons will split the skin if the swelling is so great in stops circulation. "You can see the muscles and tendons and blood vessels and everything!" she said.

"Isn't medicine wonderful," I said, adding an ironic smile.

"Oh, yes, yes!" she said, glowing. Then she looked disappointed. "I don't think they'll split your leg. The swelling is leveling off."

With that, the two physicians returned. The cardiologist smiled. "The blood work came back. It was a rattlesnake and he got you good! All your platelets are destroyed. You blood won't clot at all. Do you have ulcers?"

The internist leaned closer. "From the look of the bites I'd say you were barefoot. Where did this happen?"

"In my back yard, south of town."

He was incredulous. "You walk around the desert barefoot at night? Are you crazy or an idiot!" He leaned even closer. "I want your promise you'll never do that again! You don't get a lot of chances with this. You could lose a limb. Also, many people develop a killer allergy to rattlesnake antivenin, and you have used up your luck!" That was it, except for watching my foot turn black, and there was no tissue loss. It was almost certainly a Mojave rattler, and my foot is still a little numb. I'd been lucky, even if I used it all.

A month later I watched a coiled Mojave rattler and a gopher snake near my house. The rattler did not rattle or move from its tight coil as I approached. I got a stick, kneeled, and poked it. Nothing happened, so I slid the stick under and tipped it like a cow pie. Still nothing. I let it down. Again no reaction. That snake was frozen in a defensive posture to prevent the gopher snake killing by constricting.

Kneeling there, I knew, also, that I'm no longer afraid of rattlers--just very, very, very respectful.
